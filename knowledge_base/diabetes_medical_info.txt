DIABETES MEDICAL KNOWLEDGE BASE
=================================

WHAT IS DIABETES?
Diabetes is a chronic condition that affects how your body processes blood glucose (sugar).
Type 1 diabetes: The pancreas produces little or no insulin. It requires insulin therapy.
Type 2 diabetes: The body doesn't use insulin well. It is the most common form.
Gestational diabetes: Occurs during pregnancy and usually resolves after birth.

RISK FACTORS FOR DIABETES
- Age above 45 years
- BMI greater than 25 (overweight) or greater than 30 (obese)
- Family history of diabetes
- High blood pressure (hypertension)
- History of heart disease
- Smoking history (current or former smokers have higher risk)
- Physical inactivity
- High HbA1c levels (above 5.7% indicates prediabetes, above 6.5% indicates diabetes)
- High fasting blood glucose (above 100 mg/dL indicates prediabetes)

DIAGNOSTIC CRITERIA
- Normal HbA1c: below 5.7%
- Prediabetes HbA1c: 5.7% to 6.4%
- Diabetes HbA1c: 6.5% or higher
- Normal fasting blood glucose: below 100 mg/dL
- Prediabetes fasting glucose: 100 to 125 mg/dL
- Diabetes fasting glucose: 126 mg/dL or higher

BLOOD GLUCOSE LEVELS
- Normal blood glucose (fasting): 70–99 mg/dL
- Prediabetes: 100–125 mg/dL
- Diabetes: 126 mg/dL or above
- Post-meal (2 hours): below 140 mg/dL is normal

HbA1c EXPLAINED
HbA1c (glycated hemoglobin) measures average blood sugar over 2–3 months.
It is expressed as a percentage. Higher values mean poorer blood sugar control.
Doctors use HbA1c to diagnose and monitor diabetes management.

BMI CATEGORIES
- Underweight: BMI below 18.5
- Normal weight: BMI 18.5 to 24.9
- Overweight: BMI 25 to 29.9
- Obese: BMI 30 or above

TREATMENT OPTIONS
Type 2 diabetes is managed with lifestyle changes, oral medications (metformin), 
and sometimes insulin. Regular exercise, healthy diet, and weight loss can 
reverse prediabetes and improve type 2 diabetes outcomes.

COMPLICATIONS OF UNCONTROLLED DIABETES
- Cardiovascular disease and stroke
- Kidney damage (nephropathy)
- Eye damage (retinopathy) and blindness
- Nerve damage (neuropathy)
- Foot problems and amputations
- Increased infection risk

PREVENTION TIPS
- Maintain healthy body weight
- Exercise at least 150 minutes per week
- Eat a balanced, low-sugar diet
- Avoid smoking
- Regular blood sugar monitoring
- Annual health check-ups for at-risk individuals
